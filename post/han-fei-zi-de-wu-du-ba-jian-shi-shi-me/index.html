The Five Vermin and The Eight Treacheries

2025-12-02 history

Han Feizi’s Five Vermin and Eight Treacheries

Han Feizi (c. 280–233 BCE) was a foundational thinker of Legalism, a pragmatic school of ancient Chinese philosophy that emphasized strict laws, centralized authority, and strong statecraft to maintain social order and strengthen the ruler’s power.

Two of his most influential concepts—Five Vermin (Wudu) and Eight Treacheries (Bajian)—target the groups and behaviors he deemed destructive to a stable, prosperous state. These ideas shaped imperial governance in China for centuries and remain relevant to discussions of political authority and social control.

1. The Five Vermin (Wudu)

The term “vermin” metaphorically refers to five social groups that Han Feizi argued erode the state’s power by prioritizing their own interests over collective stability. He believed these groups undermined the rule of law and military strength—two pillars of a strong Legalist state.

  1. Scholars (Ru)
    These were Confucian scholars who advocated for traditional morality, ritual, and the teachings of the past. Han Feizi criticized them for distracting rulers with idealistic theories instead of practical laws; he claimed their emphasis on “virtue” weakened the state’s ability to enforce strict punishments and rewards.

  2. Speechmakers (Xianshuo)
    Refers to itinerant persuaders, diplomats, and debaters who traveled between states to offer political advice or form alliances. Han Feizi viewed them as opportunists who manipulated rulers with empty rhetoric, sowing discord and undermining centralized decision-making.

  3. Swordsmen (Cike)
    These were independent warriors, assassins, or vigilantes who followed a code of personal loyalty rather than state law. Han Feizi argued that their private feuds and vigilante justice challenged the ruler’s monopoly on force, creating a culture of lawlessness.

  4. Private Clerks (Sishu)
    Refers to officials who served local lords or wealthy families instead of the central government. Han Feizi condemned them for siphoning power from the state, as they prioritized the interests of their patrons over the ruler’s authority.

  5. Merchants and Artisans (Shanggong)
    While farmers were the backbone of the state (producing food and paying taxes), Han Feizi viewed merchants and artisans as non-productive. He argued that merchants accumulated wealth through trade rather than labor, while artisans focused on luxury goods—both groups drained resources from agriculture and weakened the state’s economic foundation.

2. The Eight Treacheries (Bajian)

The Eight Treacheries are eight tactics or schemes that Han Feizi warned could be used by court officials, relatives, or subordinates to usurp the ruler’s power. Unlike the Five Vermin (a critique of social groups), the Eight Treacheries target the inner workings of the court and the ruler’s vulnerability to manipulation.

  1. Flattering the ruler’s whims
    Subordinates who cater to the ruler’s personal desires (e.g., luxury, pleasure) instead of advising him on state interests, gradually gaining influence to manipulate policy.

  2. Misusing the ruler’s relatives
    Exploiting the ruler’s trust in family members (e.g., princes, empresses) to gain access to power or block reformist policies.

  3. Bribing the ruler’s attendants
    Corrupting the ruler’s servants, eunuchs, or advisors to spread false information or slander political rivals.

  4. Cultivating private factions
    Building alliances with other officials, generals, or nobles to form a power bloc that challenges the ruler’s authority.

  5. Trading official positions for personal gain
    Selling government posts to wealthy or influential individuals, undermining merit-based appointments and eroding public trust in the state.

  6. Indulging the ruler in vice
    Luring the ruler into excessive drinking, hunting, or sexual pleasure to distract him from governance, allowing subordinates to seize power in his absence.

  7. Slandering loyal ministers
    Spreading false accusations against honest officials to eliminate rivals and consolidate one’s own power.

  8. Misappropriating military power
    Tricking the ruler into granting military command, then using the army to threaten the throne or wage private wars.

Core Significance for English Readers

For Western audiences, Han Feizi’s Five Vermin and Eight Treacheries offer a window into ancient Chinese political realism—a stark contrast to the so called idealistic governance models of classical Greek philosophy (e.g., Plato’s Republic).
Key takeaways include:

  • Rejection of moral governance: Unlike Confucianism or Western natural law theory, Legalism argues that order comes from strict laws, not virtuous rulers or citizens.
  • Centralization of power: Han Feizi’s ideas reflect a universal tension between individual freedom and state authority—a theme still debated in modern politics (e.g., debates over civil liberties vs. national security).
  • Critique of special interests: The Five Vermin highlight how lobbying, rhetoric, or elite privilege can undermine state efficiency—a concept familiar to discussions of modern political corruption.
